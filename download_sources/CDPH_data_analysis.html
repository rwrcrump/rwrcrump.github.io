CDPH Data Analysis

Robert Crump

18 October, 2021

Executive Summary

The COVID-19 pandemic profoundly impacted Chicago, particularly communities of color on the South and West sides. As of October 11th 2021, COVID-19 has claimed 6003 lives, mostly in majority Black and Hispanic neighborhoods and among people over 60 years old. This analysis uses City of Chicago Department of Public Health data to generate a high-level over view of pandemic trends and assess the effectiveness of vaccination rollouts in preventing death among the most vulnerable populations.

Key Findings

  • Covid deaths are varied over time, characterized by major spikes

  • 41% of covid deaths were Black, 32% were Latinx

  • 79% of covid deaths were over 60 years old

  • Although vaccine deployment was done in phases, the initial introduction of the vaccine to healthcare workers was most strongly correlated with declines in covid deaths among vulnerable populations

  • The first phase of the vaccine deployment resulted in 3.16 fewer covid deaths per day for people 60 and older, 4.7 fewer for Black Chicagoans 60 and over, and 0.28 fewer for Latinx Chicagoans 60 and over.

Data

I retrieved this data from the City of Chicago open data portal1. This dataset contains the rolling average of cases, deaths, and hospitalization totals rates (per 100,00 people) for every seven-day period ending on each date. Every row is a single day, and 97 columns comprise various demographic categories. For this analysis, I focus on death counts and rates by age and race.

This dataset treats both age and race as categorical variables. Age variables are divided into brackets: 0-17, 18-29, 30-39, etc. Its been well documented that older adults are most vulnerable to serious illness and death after getting infected with COVID-19. To simplify the analysis, I grouped age brackets into two categories: under 60 and 60 & over.

Statistical analysis of vaccine efficacy

Beginning in December 2020, the City of Chicago began distributing vaccines to front-line healthcare workers. Over the next several months, the City introduced a phased distribution that prioritized older residents and co-morbidities2.

  • Phase 1A: Healthcare & longterm residential care workers
    • December 15th, 2020
  • Phase 1B: Over 65 years old, most essential workers, shelters & prisons
    • January 25, 2021
  • Phase 1C: 16-64 years old with co-morbidities, all essential workers
    • March 29, 2021
  • Phase 2: All Chicagoans over 16 years old
    • April 19, 2021

Phase 1B starting on Jan 25, 2021 provides a clear marker in time to assess vaccine efficacy among vulnerable populations. Although it does not match perfectly with age brackets in our data, its close enough to evaluate the impact of vaccination on the 60 & over population. Since distribution takes time and immunization is delayed about two weeks after treatment, we should consider an appropriate range of time around vaccine deployment to properly assess population health trends. To capture pre and post trends around Jan 25, I subsetted the data to include only death counts that fall within December 1, 2020 and March 31st, 2021.

The regression table shows statistically significant negative effects of vaccine deployment among each demographic subset, which are also illustrated in the chart above. I was expecting to see a regression discontinuity around Phase 1B, but the slope of the regression line remains rather constant on either side of the Jan 25 threshold. This indicates that the downward trend started earlier. Lets try the same thing, but looking at the timeframe around Phase 1A when the vaccine was made available to frontline healthcare workers. Its also worth noting that the second largest spike in cases, hospitalizations, and deaths occurred around the holiday season, so the downward slope following that period may be at least partially attributable to trends stabilizing after a peak.

By pushing back our timeframe by one month, we can see a very clear shift in covid deaths among each demographic population subset just before the vaccine was deployed to medical staff. I think there are a few key takeaways evidenced by this chart. First, the downward trend started before vaccines came online. We see a peak between Thanksgiving and Christmas, then a steep decline heading into 2021. Infection and death rates spiked when people gathered for Thanksgiving, but started to drop afterwards. Second, once vaccines were introduced into the population through nurses and other healthcare professionals, the downward trend continued instead of spiking again after Christmas.

There is another potentially disturbing implication in this data, although I cannot comment on its validity without more research and collaboration. The demographics shown in these charts dont include an occupational subset for the healthcare industry. But, deploying vaccines to them dramatically decreased covid deaths among the most vulnerable populations. One potential way to interpret this data is that frontline healthcare workers are major disease vectors themselves. The first two key takeaways point towards some counterarguments. Factors outside of vaccination (like policy and behavior) that impact transmission rates which is why we see a huge spike right before lockdowns went into effect in Spring 2020. Also, introducing vaccinations into the general population at any level may have had a similar effect. Once a virus encounters an inoculated person, it is less likely to spread to another person.

Finally, lets estimate vaccination efficacy on the demographics in question using a regression discontinuity design (RDD) around Phase 1A. The regression table below was created by changing the date variable into a binary (before and after December 15, 2020) and interacting it with racial demographics variables. The dependent variable is death counts for people 60 and over on a given day between December 1, 2020 and March 31, 2021. The RDD specification is defined by the equation below:

\[Y_i = \alpha + \tau V_i * \beta X_i + \epsilon_i\] Where \(i\) represents a given day during Phase 1A, \(Y\) represents deaths among people 60 and older, \(\alpha\) represents the constant, \(\tau\) represents the magnitude effect of vaccine availability \(V\), \(\beta\) represents the magnitude effect of belonging to a racial demographic \(X\), and \(\epsilon\) represents the error term.
